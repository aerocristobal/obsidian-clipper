          Fewer federal workers, same mission: Why AI is the productivity-first technology critical to agency operations and efficiency

          AI is the mission-critical tool that can drive new levels of efficiency, enabling agencies to do more with fewer workers yet still deliver for Americans.

          Scott Francis
          April 16, 2026 5:09 pm
          4 min read
               

          Despite promises by the Department of Government Efficiency to reduce government spending by $1 trillion before October 2025, their efforts fell short. According to deep reporting by The New York Times, DOGE either misreported or were just wrong about some of the largest savings they claimed. In short: DOGE made many smaller cuts impacting small American companies, which, when compared with the massive scale of the U.S. federal budget, did little to impact overall federal spending.

          And, while the perception of DOGE-driven deep cuts to government spending persists today, it actually increased, especially on technology. According to analysis from Deltek, in 2024, federal agencies spent $126 billion on IT contracts, up from $120 billion in 2023.

          One way DOGE did transform government was through its efforts to downsize the federal workforce. Over 317,000 employees left the federal government through layoffs, firings and retirement buyouts last year. The impact of massive downsizing of our federal workforce may not be felt immediately; however, the remaining employees are sure to be reeling from the extra workload they’re now responsible for.

          Investing in AI and the future

          Despite the severe budget cuts and “downsizing,” government agencies must still deliver on the promise to serve Americans quickly and efficiently. Doing so with less workers may prove to be challenging for many agencies. One area the federal government is investing in is AI. Deltek reported that federal spending on AI and AI-related technologies is projected to rise by 15%, increasing from $2.7 billion in 2026 to $3.1 billion in 2028.

          The government aims to compete more aggressively in an AI-driven world by filling technology skills gaps, according to a December letter from Office of Personnel Management Director Scott Kupor. While OPM is making a concerted effort to find and hire top-tier technology talent, which is critically important, there are more than two million civilian employees that can leverage AI to do more with less by automating routine and time-consuming tasks.

          The good news is the federal government realizes the value of AI, and its use is increasing: The Government Accountability Office found that the total number of AI use cases across 11 agencies nearly doubled in 2024 over the previous year, while generative AI use increased nine times over the same time period.

          The public sector tends to lag behind the private sector when it comes to adopting new technologies and processes. But agencies would be remiss if they didn’t make AI tools available to employees to drive better outcomes now. It goes without saying that federal agencies will need to equip teams with the skills they need to get the most out of the AI tools they employ and provide the necessary guidance on approved uses. Some agencies have their own internal learning platforms, while some contract with LinkedIn Learning and Coursera, two of the most well-regarded platforms for employee upskilling.

          Three ways agencies can incorporate AI into daily workflows, score quick wins, and almost immediately see return on AI investments, include:

          • Leverage FADGI-compliant document scanners for paper record digitization. The federal government has been making baby steps towards modernization and digitization. One initiative that helped move the needle is the Federal Agencies Digital Guidelines Initiative (FADGI) which supports federal mandate M-23-07. This mandate requires all paper-based permanent records to be submitted to the National Archives and Records Administration with a FADGI-compliant digital scanner. The image quality requirements for FADGI ensure that each record is captured in its true fidelity, which is essential for accurate AI processing and analysis.
          • Maintaining high level output by reducing low level tasks. FADGI is an important step toward digitalization, but to take advantage of the digitization of data and documents into fully integrated systems that connect data, decision-making and operations across the entire organization, AI is imperative. It’s the most effective way to automate high volume, repetitive tasks, including document routing requests, basic form processing, managing compliance and even standard intra-agency communications. AI empowers smaller teams to maintain the same high-level throughput because lower-level tasks are now handled by machines. This becomes mission critical when there’s fewer team members to tackle the same workloads.
          • Making knowledge management more intelligent. Unlike legacy knowledge management (KM) systems, intelligent knowledge management (IKM) utilizes AI, machine learning, and natural language processing (NLP) to automate the analysis and structure of information. It then derives insights from a vast amount of data to automate the knowledge retrieval and decision-making process. For example, as employees leave their positions, they take the agency’s institutional knowledge with them. IKM enables remaining staff to instantly access this dark data and gives them a searchable “memory” of the organization. This reduces the time spent looking for information and may reduce onboarding time for reassigned employees.

          The AI imperative

          AI is the mission-critical tool that can drive new levels of efficiency, enabling agencies to do more with fewer workers yet still deliver on the promises made to the American people. AI’s ability to automate routine tasks and streamline workflows can free up workers to focus on higher-value responsibilities. More importantly, with strategic and thoughtful implementation, AI can help fill the gaps left by severe and sudden workforce reduction and ease the burden on our country’s federal workforce.

          Scott Francis is a technology evangelist at PFU America, Inc.
